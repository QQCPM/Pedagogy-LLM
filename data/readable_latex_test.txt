# Gradient Descent

## Intuitive Understanding

Imagine you're lost in a hilly landscape, and you want to get to the lowest point in the valley. You can't see the entire valley at once; you can only see the ground immediately around you. A reasonable strategy would be to look around and take a step in the direction where the ground slopes downwards most steeply.  Gradient descent is analogous to this process. We're trying to find the "lowest point" (minimum) of a function, and we repeatedly take steps in the direction of the steepest descent. The "steepness" is represented by the gradient.

## Mathematical Definition

Let $J(\theta)$ be a scalar-valued function, where $\theta$ represents the parameters we want to optimize (e.g., weights in a machine learning model).  The gradient of $J$ with respect to $\theta$, denoted by $\nabla J(\theta)$, is a vector of partial derivatives:

$$
\nabla J(\theta) = \begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial J}{\partial \theta_1} \\
\frac{\partial J}{\partial \theta_2} \\
\vdots \\
\frac{\partial J}{\partial \theta_n}
\end{bmatrix}
$$

The gradient descent update rule is:

$$
\theta := \theta - \eta \nabla J(\theta)
$$

where:
* $\theta$ is the vector of parameters.
* $\eta$ (eta) is the learning rate, a positive scalar controlling the step size.
* $\nabla J(\theta)$ is the gradient of the cost function $J$ with respect to $\theta$.

The update rule essentially moves the parameters $\theta$ in the opposite direction of the gradient, with a step size determined by the learning rate $\eta$.

## Step-by-step Example

Let's consider a simple cost function:

$$
J(\theta) = \theta^2 + 5\theta + 10
$$

We want to find the value of $\theta$ that minimizes $J(\theta)$.

1. **Calculate the Gradient:**

The gradient of $J(\theta)$ with respect to $\theta$ is:

$$
\frac{\partial J}{\partial \theta} = 2\theta + 5
$$

2. **Initialize Parameters and Learning Rate:**

Let's initialize $\theta$ to 3 and set the learning rate $\eta$ to 0.1.

$$
\theta_0 = 3, \quad \eta = 0.1
$$

3. **Perform one Gradient Descent Step:**

Using the gradient descent update rule:

$$
\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \frac{\partial J}{\partial \theta}
$$

Plugging in the values:

$$
\theta_1 = 3 - 0.1(2(3) + 5) = 3 - 0.1(11) = 3 - 1.1 = 1.9
$$

4. **Perform another step:**

$$
\theta_2 = 1.9 - 0.1(2(1.9) + 5) = 1.9 - 0.1(3.8 + 5) = 1.9 - 0.1(8.8) = 1.9 - 0.88 = 1.02
$$

We continue this process until the change in $\theta$ becomes sufficiently small, or a maximum number of iterations is reached.  In each iteration, we are moving closer to the minimum of the function.

## Why This Matters

Gradient descent is a cornerstone algorithm in machine learning and optimization. It's used to train a vast array of models, including:

* **Linear Regression:** Finding the best-fit line.
* **Logistic Regression:** Training classification models.
* **Neural Networks:** Optimizing weights and biases to minimize prediction error.
* **Support Vector Machines (SVMs):** Finding the optimal hyperplane.

The ability to efficiently minimize complex cost functions is crucial for building effective machine learning models.

## Connection to Other Concepts

* **Taylor Series:** Gradient descent is based on the first-order Taylor approximation of a function. The gradient represents the direction of the steepest increase, so moving in the opposite direction minimizes the function.
* **Newton's Method:** A second-order optimization algorithm that uses both the gradient and the Hessian matrix (matrix of second derivatives) to find the minimum. It often converges faster than gradient descent, but requires computing the Hessian, which can be computationally expensive.
* **Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD):** A variant of gradient descent that uses a single data point (or a small batch) to estimate the gradient, making it faster for large datasets.
* **Momentum:**  A technique to accelerate gradient descent by adding a "momentum" term that accumulates past gradients.  This helps to overcome local minima and speed up convergence.
$$ v_t = \gamma v_{t-1} + \eta \nabla J(\theta) $$ where $\gamma$ is the momentum coefficient.

# Multivariate Normal Distribution Probability Density Function

## Intuitive Understanding

Imagine you're tracking the location of a bee in a garden. The bee's position isn't perfectly predictable; it tends to cluster around a central point (let's say the flowerbed). However, it can wander off in any direction. The multivariate normal distribution describes this kind of situation, where a random variable has several components, and each component is normally distributed, with the components being correlated.  The "multivariate" part simply means we're dealing with more than one variable at a time.  The distribution tells us the probability of the bee being at a specific location in the garden, given its tendency to cluster around the flowerbed.

## Mathematical Definition

Let $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ \vdots \\ x_n \end{bmatrix}$ be a random vector, where $x_i$ are random variables. The multivariate normal distribution with mean vector $\mu = \begin{bmatrix} \mu_1 \\ \mu_2 \\ \vdots \\ \mu_n \end{bmatrix}$ and covariance matrix $\Sigma$ (a symmetric, positive-definite matrix) has a probability density function (PDF) given by:

$$
f(\mathbf{x} ; \mu, \Sigma) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{n/2} |\Sigma|^{1/2}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} (\mathbf{x} - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \mu)}
$$

Where:

*   $\mathbf{x}$ is the random vector.
*   $\mu$ is the mean vector, representing the center of the distribution.
*   $\Sigma$ is the covariance matrix, describing the spread and correlation of the variables.
*   $|\Sigma|$ denotes the determinant of the covariance matrix $\Sigma$.
*   $\Sigma^{-1}$ is the inverse of the covariance matrix.
*   $(\mathbf{x} - \mu)^T$ is the transpose of the vector $(\mathbf{x} - \mu)$.
*   $e$ is Euler's number, approximately 2.71828.
*   $n$ is the dimension of the random vector $\mathbf{x}$.

## Step-by-step Example

Let's consider a bivariate normal distribution (n = 2) with mean $\mu = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$, covariance matrix $\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$, and we want to evaluate the PDF at the point $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 3.5 \end{bmatrix}$.

1.  **Calculate $(\mathbf{x} - \mu)$:**
    $$
    \mathbf{x} - \mu = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 3.5 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix}
    $$

2.  **Calculate $(\mathbf{x} - \mu)^T$:**
    $$
    (\mathbf{x} - \mu)^T = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0.5 \end{bmatrix}
    $$

3.  **Calculate $\Sigma^{-1}$:**
    Since $\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0.5 \\ 0.5 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$, the inverse is $\Sigma^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 \\ -0.5 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

4.  **Calculate $(\mathbf{x} - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \mu)$:**
    $$
    \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0.5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 \\ -0.5 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -0.5 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0.5 \end{bmatrix} = 1 - 0.25 = 0.75
    $$

5.  **Calculate the determinant of $\Sigma$:**
    $$
    |\Sigma| = (1)(1) - (0.5)(0.5) = 1 - 0.25 = 0.75
    $$

6.  **Plug everything into the PDF:**
    $$
    f(\mathbf{x} ; \mu, \Sigma) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{2/2} (0.75)^{1/2}} e^{-\frac{1}{2} (0.75)} = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{1} (0.75)^{1/2}} e^{-0.375} \approx \frac{1}{6.28 \times 0.866} e^{-0.375} \approx \frac{1}{5.434} e^{-0.375} \approx 0.184 \times 0.687 = 0.126
    $$
    So, $f(\mathbf{x} ; \mu, \Sigma) \approx 0.126$.

## Why This Matters

The multivariate normal distribution is a cornerstone of many fields:

*   **Statistics:** It's frequently used as a model for complex data sets.
*   **Machine Learning:** Used in Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) and Bayesian methods.
*   **Finance:** Used to model asset returns and portfolio risk.
*   **Image Processing:**  Used in various image analysis techniques.
*   **Engineering:** Used in signal processing and control systems.

## Connection to Other Concepts

*   **Normal Distribution (Gaussian Distribution):** The multivariate normal distribution is a generalization of the univariate normal distribution to multiple dimensions.  A multivariate normal distribution can be thought of as a collection of correlated univariate normal distributions.
*   **Covariance:** The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ describes the relationships between the different variables.  Diagonal elements represent the variances of individual variables, while off-diagonal elements represent the covariances between them.
*   **Linear Algebra:** The PDF involves matrix operations like determinants and matrix inversion, connecting it to linear algebra concepts.
*   **Bayes' Theorem:**  The multivariate normal distribution frequently appears in Bayesian inference as a prior or likelihood distribution.

# Least Squares Solution

## Intuitive Understanding

Imagine you're trying to fit a straight line through a set of data points.  Sometimes, the line won't perfectly pass through any of the points. The "least squares" method finds the line that minimizes the *sum of the squared vertical distances* between the line and the data points.  In other words, we're trying to find the line that makes the "errors" as small as possible, where the magnitude of each error is squared.  This squaring penalizes larger errors more heavily, leading to a good overall fit.  The same principle extends to fitting more complex functions to data ‚Äì we are minimizing the squared error.

## Mathematical Definition

Let $\mathbf{A}$ be an $m \times n$ matrix, $\mathbf{b}$ be an $m \times 1$ vector, and $\mathbf{x}$ be an $n \times 1$ vector.  We want to find the vector $\mathbf{x}$ that minimizes the Euclidean norm of the residual vector $\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{Ax}$.  The residual vector represents the difference between the actual values (represented by $\mathbf{b}$) and the predicted values (represented by $\mathbf{Ax}$).

The sum of squared errors, denoted by $J(\mathbf{x})$, is given by:

$$J(\mathbf{x}) = \|\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{Ax}\|^2$$

To minimize $J(\mathbf{x})$, we take its derivative with respect to $\mathbf{x}$ and set it equal to zero.  Using the chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial J}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = 2(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{Ax})^T (-\mathbf{A})$$

Setting the derivative to zero gives us the normal equations:

$$2(\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{Ax})^T (-\mathbf{A}) = \mathbf{0}$$

Multiplying by $-\frac{1}{2}$ and rearranging, we get:

$$\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{b}$$

If $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A}$ is invertible, then the least squares solution is given by:

$$\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A})^{-1} \mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{b}$$

If $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A}$ is not invertible (i.e., it is singular), we can use the pseudoinverse, denoted by $(\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A})^+$, to find a solution:

$$\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A})^+ \mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{b}$$

## Step-by-step Example

Let's consider a simple example:

Suppose we have the following data points: $(1, 2)$, $(2, 1)$, $(3, 3)$. We want to fit a simple linear model $y = ax + b$ to these data points.

We can represent this in matrix form as:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} \rightarrow ax + b
$$

We can rewrite this as:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}
$$

Here, $\mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$, $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix}$, and $\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$.

Now, we calculate $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A}$:

$$
\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \\ 3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 14 & 6 \\ 6 & 3 \end{bmatrix}
$$

Next, we calculate $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{b}$:

$$
\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 14 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}
$$

Now, we need to solve the system:

$$
\begin{bmatrix} 14 & 6 \\ 6 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a \\ b \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 14 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix}
$$

The inverse of $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A}$ is:

$$
(\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A})^{-1} = \frac{1}{(14)(3) - (6)^2} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -6 & 14 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{12} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -6 \\ -6 & 14 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{4} & -\frac{1}{2} \\ -\frac{1}{2} & \frac{7}{6} \end{bmatrix}
$$

Finally, we calculate the least squares solution:

$$
\mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A})^{-1} \mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{4} & -\frac{1}{2} \\ -\frac{1}{2} & \frac{7}{6} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 14 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{14}{4} - \frac{6}{2} \\ -\frac{14}{2} + \frac{42}{6} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{7}{2} - 3 \\ -7 + 7 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}
$$

Therefore, $a = \frac{1}{2}$ and $b = 0$.  Our linear model is $y = \frac{1}{2}x$.

## Why This Matters

The least squares method is fundamental in many fields:

*   **Statistics:**  Regression analysis relies heavily on least squares.
*   **Machine Learning:** Used in linear regression, principal component analysis (PCA), and other algorithms.
*   **Engineering:**  Used in signal processing, control systems, and optimization problems.
*   **Geophysics:** Used to determine the best fit to geological data.

## Connection to Other Concepts

*   **Linear Algebra:** The least squares solution is deeply rooted in linear algebra concepts like matrix inversion, eigenvalues, and eigenvectors.  The condition number of $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A}$ is an important indicator of the sensitivity of the solution to errors in the data.
*   **Calculus:**  The derivation of the least squares solution involves taking derivatives and setting them equal to zero ‚Äì a core concept in multivariable calculus.
*   **Gradient Descent:** Gradient descent is an iterative optimization algorithm that can be used to find the least squares solution.
*   **Singular Value Decomposition (SVD):** SVD provides a powerful way to solve least squares problems, especially when $\mathbf{A}^T \mathbf{A}$ is singular. It provides a more numerically stable solution than direct inversion.
